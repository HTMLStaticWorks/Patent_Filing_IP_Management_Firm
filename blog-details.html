The Impact of Amgen v. Sanofi on Enablement Requirements

Jonathan Reed, Esq.
Oct 12, 2026 • 8 min read

The Supreme Court's recent unanimous decision in Amgen Inc. v. Sanofi has fundamentally shifted how patent practitioners must approach the drafting of broad genus claims, particularly in the unpredictable arts such as biotechnology and pharmaceuticals.

The Core Issue: The "Wands Factors" Re-examined

At the heart of the dispute was whether Amgen's patent claims, which encompassed an entire genus of antibodies that bind to specific amino acid residues on the PCSK9 protein, met the enablement requirement under 35 U.S.C. § 112(a).

The Court held that the broader the monopoly claimed, the more detailed the disclosure must be to enable a person of ordinary skill in the art (POSITA) to make and use the entire scope of the claimed invention without "undue experimentation."

What This Means for Prosecution Strategy

Going forward, applicants cannot rely on functional claiming to cover millions of possible combinations if they only disclose a handful of specific examples. The specification must act as a roadmap.

At Novus IP, we are advising our biotech clients to:

  • File narrower "picture claims" covering the exact commercial embodiment.
  • Provide robust, structurally diverse examples across the claimed genus.
  • Avoid pure functional claiming unless tied to a highly predictable structural correlation.
